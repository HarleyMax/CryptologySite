The Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange is a cryptographic system in which two parties first agree on two numbers, p and g. p is any prime number, but preferably a large one to prevent attacks. g is a number that is relatively prime to p, meaning that the only factor that g and p share is 1. Each party then decides on their own secret number that is less than p. These secret numbers are represented by "a" and "b". Now that the parties have set all of these parameters, calculations can begin. Two public keys are generated with the formulas A = ga mod p and B = gb mod p. Both parties can then obtain the mutual secret key by calculating Ab mod p and Ba mod p respectively.
